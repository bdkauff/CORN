Hello!


From the toothpaste you use in the morning to the book you read at night, corn plays a part in nearly every aspect of our lives.
-corn.org


Corn comes in many forms. Toothpaste corn, for instance, is Sorbitol, 'synthesized by sorbitol-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, and converted to fructose by succinate dehydrogenase and sorbitol dehydrogenase.' Corn comes in drywall, in diapers, and in 3d printers.

Despite this, the majority of our submissions to CORN include corn in its most recognizable, iconic on-the-cob incarnation. This corn-- sweet, yellow, and uniform-- has an lovable immediacy. It's the corn we literally and figuratively internalize.

Corn on the cob is familiar. Corn on the cob is nostalgic. Corn on the cob is fun. It's fun to eat. It's fun to say. Corn on the cob.

Corn...would not exist if it weren't for the humans that cultivated and developed it. It is a human invention, a plant that does not exist naturally in the wild. It can only survive if planted and protected by humans.
-camp silos


After just a couple shucks corn gets complicated. Corn asks: when does growth become overgrowth? Corn is often both the problem and the solution. Corn problematizes the problem/solution model. Corn is the food for our food. Corn is even the medicine for the food for our food.

Known corn, loved corn, the hot-buttered just-off-the-grill kind, makes up such a narrow chapter of corn's history. Corn unknown spreads in a wild control, insidiously, getting bigger, taller, will maybe end with a real big pop.

In the meantime, a corny reminder: you're a kernel on a cob, in a field of cobs, on a planet covered, knowingly or not.
