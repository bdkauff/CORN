Cornfield with Saints

Steven Lance

+++
Traveling this summer in rural France, I was surprised to find cornfields pretty much everywhere I went.  Cornfields in Provence full of barking cicadas, cornfields in Lorraine ringed with pine trees and a few old Front Nationale posters, cornfields in the plain of Alsace all the way to the border.  I had always thought of cornfields as distinctly American, like baseball socks or free coffee refills.  Seeing those same yellow-tufted landscapes in a foreign country was a little uncanny.  (This to say nothing of Buffalo Grill, a western-themed steakhouse that seems to be a big hit in France.)

There’s some basis for a patriotic response like mine – the United States is by far the world’s largest producer and consumer of corn, and corn has been grown as a cereal crop in the Americas for thousands of years.  But a few google searches have made it clear that the western hemisphere lost its monopoly on cornfields a long time ago.  According to an Iowa State University web page on the “Origins, History, and Uses of Corn,” Columbus brought corn to Europe after either his first or second voyage to the new world.  Planted first near Seville in 1494, corn soon made its way across the continent, reaching China and the Philippines within a century.  



Late one afternoon in August, I took a picture of three statues of saints in a cornfield.  Judging from other photos on the same roll of film, it must have been somewhere between Toulouse and Cahors, in the Lot department of southwestern France.  They seemed incongruous, besieged behind a low fence, framed by telephone wires overhead and the asphalt of a little-traveled route départementale a few steps below.  I remember thinking they must have been designed for other surroundings that fit them better, lost over the years and replaced by corn, this alien grain.  Their encirclement seemed to typify the fate of a formerly significant monument that loses its context and stops making sense.  

But I wasn’t quite right: the context may never have changed.  Corn could easily have been growing in those fields long before the statues showed up.  Corn has been a French crop since at least 1612, a corn-industry website called MaïsCultureDurable.com tells me, when its presence is attested in a notary act for the commune of Montpont-en-Bresse in Burgundy.  Today, according to the Association Générale des Producteurs de Maïs (AGPM), France produces 18 million metric tons of corn annually and “stands as a force confronting the American giant.”  This confrontation is framed as heroic but unwinnable: “Les Etats Unis, rois du maïs” produce 41% of the world’s corn to France’s 3%.  French corn farmers were initially reluctant to follow the Americans in planting higher-yield hybrids, the AGPM’s website explains, “but hybrids did finally prevail, as was only logical, in the 1950s.”  Under another heading, the site’s authors declare ominously that “with French regulations on GMOs, the country is now lagging behind.”

This brings me back to the saints in the cornfield.  If their context hasn’t changed that much, if they were perhaps designed for that cornfield and maybe even displaced a few cornstalks when they were erected, what do they mean?  The crowned figure in the center is probably the Virgin Mary.  That may be Joan of Arc on the left in armor, and the bald, male figure on the right seems to be some kind of priest.  On the Virgin’s pedestal are the dates 1858-1908 and a Latin motto which the internet translates as “They have made me their protector.”  Whom did she protect?  Did pious landowners build these statues to bring good harvests, to show gratitude for someone’s miraculous recovery, to discourage passers-by from helping themselves to a few ears of protected corn?  Were these saintly bodies warding off leaf blight in the days before hybrids and pesticides?  And why the years 1858 to 1908?  50 years is not too short to be someone’s lifetime – do the saints mark a grave?


That day in August, running late and partly lost, I didn’t stay long enough at the cornfield to understand the group of stone figures.  For me it was just more corn along a highway, and another half-neglected roadside shrine.  Now October is almost here and the corn stalks I saw will have grown taller, probably surpassing at least the armored woman and the priest, if not the virgin herself.  In the next month the corn will be harvested and the fields left bare.  The saints will wait out winter alone.  I guess this rhythm can continue forever: the corn will be planted in April, harvested in October, and heavy farm machinery will hiss and scrape against the statues’ protective fencing in both seasons.  In another hundred years perhaps a tougher, taller hybrid or GMO strain will replace the corn we recognize.  If the highway doesn’t get widened and force their removal, the saints will see all of this.  They’ll watch over these new beings, Joan with her lance and armor, the virgin with her prayers and sacred melancholy, the priest with his incantations; but they’ll seem smaller compared to the corn, and even more alien.
